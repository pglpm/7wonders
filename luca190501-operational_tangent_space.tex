\section{***}
\label{sec:***}

Imagine to stand on a differential manifold and to be armed with a pen and
with some kind of instrument that can tell you the value of every function
and of the parameter of every curve at each point of the manifold.

For a being thus equipped, what is a tangent vector? how is the sum of two
tangent vectors performed? and what about other tangent objects and other
operations with them?

We know that a differential manifold locally looks like $\RR^{n}$, by
definition. We know the various definitions of tangent vector and tangent
space. The latter is sometimes represented as a portion of $\RR^{n}$ -- for
example, a portion of a plane -- tangent to a point of the manifold. But
when we \enquote{stand} on that point there's no such plane to be seen.
Moreover, how can we conceive of straight lines, even very short ones, if
we only have a notion of smoothness and no notions of parallelism or
distance?

In this note I present, very informally, a practical way to think about
tangent vectors, tangent covectors, and their operations; and about some
other tangent objects and their operations. It may help students of
differential geometry get an intuitive and visual understanding of tangent
objects. It's a way of thinking inspired by the illuminating graphical
representation discussed by Burke \citep[\sect~II.8, especially
pp.~62--65]{burke1985_r1987}. Unlike Burke I won't introduce coordinate
charts. In fact, the notion of chart will naturally appear, as a derived
object, from the way of thinking presented here; this derivation bears some
similarities with Chevalley's \citey[\chap~III]{chevalley1946} and Penrose
\amp\ Rindler's \citey[\sect~4.1]{penroseetal1984_r2003} definition of
differential manifold via a ring of functions (compare the first footnote
on p.~181 of the last reference).
